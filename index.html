01About & Now

Securing the systems that move the world.

A short orientation to my research interests, what I'm currently working on, and where I'm heading next.

I'm a Ph.D. candidate in Cybersecurity at Georgia Tech, working at the intersection of cyber-physical systems, autonomous robotics, and AI/ML hardware acceleration. My work is supported by the U.S. Department of Energy through the BIRD Energy Consortium, and is motivated by a single question — how do we keep critical infrastructure trustworthy when adversaries are already inside?

On the systems side, I design subprocess-level fault-tolerance architectures that let robotic controllers recover from active cyber-attacks within a handful of scan cycles. On the hardware side, I'm building architectures that repurpose idle DSP clusters on FPGAs into general-purpose AI accelerators — a low-cost, energy-efficient path for advanced analytics on defense-relevant compute.

Before Georgia Tech, I worked on software-supply-chain security at LUMS, where I developed a BERT-based optimization for LLVM and a debloating pipeline that cut exploitable code surface in half.

01
NOV 2025 — PRESENT
DOE · BIRD Phase 2

JBODSP — Generalized AI Acceleration via Idle DSP Clusters

Lead Researcher

A proof-of-concept FPGA architecture that repurposes parallel DSP clusters as general-purpose AI accelerators. Includes a Python-based OpenCL simulator that enforces Arria 10 memory constraints to enable rapid tile-plan validation.

  • Architecture. A three-tier parallelization framework — cross-CU dispatch, intra-CU unrolling, and temporal double-buffering — that fully overlaps DDR loads with compute, eliminating memory-access latency.
  • Selection. Competitively chosen to lead Phase 2 after Phase 1 outcomes.
  • Impact. A low-cost, energy-efficient accelerator for U.S. critical infrastructure — directly supporting DOE objectives for advanced analytics on defense-relevant hardware.
▲ Latency goal: full DDR/compute overlap on Arria 10 — zero idle compute cycles.
02
JAN 2023 — NOV 2025
DOE · BIRD Phase 1

SubprocessBFT++ — Cyber-Attack Resilience for Robotic Control Systems

Lead Researcher

A subprocess-level fault-tolerance architecture for industrial control systems that guarantees recovery within a handful of scan cycles after a control-hijacking attempt. Validated in drone simulations and generalizable to single-controller robotic platforms.

  • Generalization. Originally designed for drones, the mechanism extends to single-controller robotic platforms across domains.
  • Operational reach. Directly addresses DHS/CISA priorities for OT environments — applicable to U.S. power grids, manufacturing facilities, and autonomous-vehicle infrastructure.
  • Status. Manuscript under review at IEEE/RSJ IROS 2026.
⚡ Guaranteed recovery within 2–3 scan cycles following a control-hijacking attempt.
